Stakeholder Consultation Process

The SynD-DGF framework was developed through extensive consultation with over 150 stakeholders across healthcare, research, regulation, technology, and community sectors to ensure it meets real-world needs and reflects diverse perspectives.

Consultation process

The consultation followed a structured three-round process, with each round building on the findings from previous rounds:

Round 1: Problem identification and scoping (Months 6-8)

Objective: Understand current challenges and define framework scope

  • Stakeholder mapping workshops - Identified key players and their interests
  • Problem definition sessions - Explored current barriers and challenges
  • Current practice assessment - Documented existing approaches and gaps
  • Priority setting exercises - Determined most critical needs and use cases
Geographic coverage: Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane, Perth, Adelaide, Canberra, Darwin, Hobart

Round 2: Framework co-design (Months 9-11)

Objective: Collaboratively design framework structure and content

  • Co-design workshops - Collaborative framework development sessions
  • Technical working groups - Deep-dive sessions on specific technical aspects
  • Legal and regulatory forums - Detailed examination of compliance requirements
  • Feature prioritisation - Ranking of framework features and tools
  • Use case validation - Testing framework applicability across scenarios
Format: Mix of in-person workshops, virtual sessions, and hybrid formats to maximise participation

Round 3: Validation and refinement (Months 12-15)

Objective: Test and refine draft framework

  • Framework testing sessions - Hands-on evaluation of draft tools
  • Usability evaluation - Assessment of user experience and accessibility
  • Implementation planning - Practical considerations for adoption
  • Final feedback collection - Comprehensive review and refinement
  • Endorsement process - Formal stakeholder approval and commitment
Special focus: Indigenous consultation sessions held on Country with appropriate cultural protocols

Key consultation findings

The consultation process revealed critical insights that shaped the framework's development and priorities:

CHALLENGE

Legal complexity

Stakeholders struggled with complex and fragmented privacy laws, seeking clearer guidance on compliance requirements for synthetic data.

NEED

Practical tools

Strong demand for practical, implementable tools rather than high-level policy guidance or theoretical frameworks.

CONCERN

Indigenous data sovereignty

Critical importance of specific guidance for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander health data governance and sovereignty.

GAP

Risk assessment

Limited understanding of how to systematically assess and quantify reidentification risks in synthetic health data.

PRIORITY

Quality assurance

Need for standardised approaches to evaluate synthetic data quality and utility preservation.

BARRIER

Resource constraints

Organisations need guidance that considers limited resources and varying levels of technical expertise.

OPPORTUNITY

Innovation enablement

Framework should enable rather than hinder innovation while maintaining appropriate privacy protections.

REQUIREMENT

Regulatory alignment

Essential that framework aligns with existing regulations and supports compliance rather than creating additional burden.

"We need practical guidance that helps us navigate the complex legal landscape while enabling innovation in health research. The framework should be a roadmap, not another compliance burden."
- Chief Privacy Officer, Major Health Network
"Indigenous data sovereignty isn't just about compliance - it's about respecting our community's rights and ensuring our voices are heard in how our health data is used."
- Indigenous Health Data Governance Representative
"The framework needs to be technology-agnostic but provide specific enough guidance to be actionable. We need to know not just what to do, but how to do it."
- Data Science Team Lead, Research Institute

Consultation methods

We employed diverse consultation methods to ensure broad participation and capture different types of input:

Formal consultation methods

  • Structured workshops - Facilitated sessions with specific outcomes
  • Delphi surveys - Multi-round expert consensus building
  • Focus groups - In-depth discussions with homogeneous groups
  • Key informant interviews - One-on-one expert consultations
  • Technical working groups - Specialist deep-dive sessions
  • Public submissions - Open consultation opportunities

Inclusive engagement approaches

  • Indigenous protocols - Culturally appropriate consultation methods
  • Accessibility accommodations - Multiple format options
  • Geographic diversity - Sessions across all states and territories
  • Language support - Translation services where needed
  • Virtual participation - Online options for broader reach
  • Flexible timing - Sessions at different times to accommodate schedules

Consultation principles

All consultation activities were guided by key principles:

  • Transparency: Open process with clear documentation of inputs and outcomes
  • Inclusivity: Proactive efforts to include diverse voices and perspectives
  • Respect: Acknowledgment of stakeholder expertise and time investment
  • Responsiveness: Clear feedback on how input influenced framework development
  • Accessibility: Multiple ways to participate regardless of location or circumstances
  • Cultural safety: Appropriate protocols for Indigenous and culturally diverse participation

Indigenous consultation process

Specific consultation processes were developed to ensure appropriate engagement with Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander communities and organisations:

Cultural protocols

  • Free, prior, and informed consent - Respected community decision-making processes
  • Cultural advisors - Indigenous experts guided consultation design
  • On Country sessions - Meetings held in appropriate cultural settings
  • Traditional protocols - Acknowledgment and respect for traditional practices
  • Community ownership - Recognition of community rights over consultation outcomes

Consultation outcomes

  • Data sovereignty principles - Integration of CARE principles
  • Specific guidance - Dedicated framework sections for Indigenous data
  • Community benefit - Ensuring synthetic data serves community needs
  • Ongoing engagement - Commitment to continuing consultation relationships
  • Capacity building - Support for Indigenous data governance capabilities
"Our data is not just numbers and statistics - it carries the stories and experiences of our people. Any framework for synthetic data must respect and protect these stories while ensuring our communities benefit from research."
- Elder and Traditional Knowledge Holder
