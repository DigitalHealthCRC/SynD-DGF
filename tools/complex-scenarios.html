Complex scenarios navigator

Use this interactive decision tree when your synthetic data project doesn't fit the standard 5-step framework pathway or requires additional assessment.

When your project is "complex"

Your project requires additional assessment if any of these apply:

Cross-organisational data

  • Source data held by multiple organisations
  • Data linkage required before synthetic generation
  • Shared governance arrangements needed

Third-party involvement

  • External synthetic data generation services
  • Cloud-based processing platforms
  • International service providers

Higher privacy risks

  • Re-identification risk above "very low"
  • Highly sensitive or rare conditions
  • Small population datasets

Special populations

  • Indigenous health data
  • Vulnerable or marginalised groups
  • Children and adolescent data

Multi-organisational scenarios

Does your project require data from multiple organisations?

Additional requirements needed:

  • Data sharing agreements between all participating organisations
  • Governance framework for shared decision-making
  • Clear data custodian responsibilities
  • Unified privacy impact assessment
  • Cross-jurisdictional legal compliance review

Data linkage scenario

Your project requires linking datasets before synthetic data generation.

Critical requirements

  • Legal basis: Must establish lawful basis for data linkage under privacy laws
  • Linkage authority: May require specialised linkage unit or approved linkage protocols
  • Enhanced security: Secure linkage environment with restricted access
  • Data minimisation: Link only essential fields, remove identifiers after linkage
  • Governance oversight: Independent oversight committee or data linkage authority approval

Third-party service providers

Your project involves external service providers for synthetic data generation.

Additional risk factors to assess:

  • Data location: Where will source data be processed?
  • Provider qualifications: Technical expertise and security certifications
  • Data retention: How long will provider retain source data and models?
  • Subcontractors: Will provider engage additional parties?
  • Incident response: Provider's data breach and incident procedures

Cloud-based processing

Using cloud platforms for synthetic data generation requires enhanced safeguards.

Mandatory requirements:

  • Australian hosting: Data must remain within Australian jurisdiction
  • Encryption: End-to-end encryption for data in transit and at rest
  • Access controls: Multi-factor authentication and role-based access
  • Audit trails: Comprehensive logging of all data access and processing
  • Contractual protections: Strong data protection clauses and liability provisions
  • Regular assessments: Ongoing security and compliance reviews

Recommended next steps:

  1. Complete cloud security assessment using government frameworks
  2. Negotiate comprehensive data processing agreement
  3. Implement enhanced monitoring and assurance procedures
  4. Establish incident response and data breach protocols
  5. Consider additional insurance and liability coverage

Higher re-identification risks

Your project has re-identification risks assessed as more than "very low".

⚠️ Project must be paused

Projects with higher than very low re-identification risk cannot proceed without additional safeguards.

Required actions:

  • Enhanced de-identification: Apply stronger privacy-preserving techniques
  • Legal pathway determination: Establish lawful basis for higher-risk data processing
  • Additional safeguards: Implement enhanced security and access controls
  • Ongoing monitoring: More frequent risk reassessment and monitoring
  • Stakeholder consultation: Engage with privacy regulators and community representatives

Special populations

Your project involves data about Indigenous peoples or other special populations.

Indigenous data sovereignty requirements:

  • Community consultation: Engage with relevant Aboriginal Community Controlled Health Organisations
  • Cultural protocols: Follow established Indigenous data governance frameworks
  • Community benefit: Demonstrate clear benefit to Indigenous communities
  • Data ownership: Respect Indigenous peoples' rights to control data about their communities
  • Ethical oversight: Additional ethics review by appropriate Indigenous ethics committees

Other vulnerable populations:

  • Enhanced consent and consultation requirements
  • Additional safeguards against discrimination and stigmatisation
  • Community advocacy group engagement
  • Specialised ethics committee review

Indigenous data governance pathway

Specific requirements for projects involving Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander health data.

Mandatory consultation and approval steps:

  1. Community engagement: Early consultation with affected Indigenous communities
  2. ACCHO involvement: Engagement with relevant Aboriginal Community Controlled Health Organisations
  3. Cultural review: Assessment by Indigenous data governance experts
  4. Ethics approval: Review by Indigenous-specific ethics committee or advisory group
  5. Data sharing protocols: Agreement on appropriate data sharing and use limitations
  6. Community benefit sharing: Clear plans for sharing research benefits with communities

Key frameworks to follow:

  • NHMRC Ethical Conduct in Research with Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Peoples and Communities
  • Australian Institute of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Studies (AIATSIS) Guidelines
  • Maiam nayri Wingara Indigenous Data Sovereignty Collective principles
  • Relevant state and territory Indigenous data protocols

Completion requirements

Before proceeding, ensure all consultation and approval steps are completed and documented.

International data sharing

Your project involves sharing synthetic data with international partners or organisations.

Additional legal and regulatory considerations:

  • Cross-border restrictions: Australian privacy laws generally restrict international data transfers
  • Adequacy assessments: Evaluate privacy protections in destination countries
  • Treaty obligations: Consider any relevant international agreements or treaties
  • Foreign law compliance: Ensure compliance with destination country privacy laws
  • Enhanced safeguards: Additional contractual and technical protections may be required

Recommended approach:

  1. Conduct detailed legal analysis of cross-border transfer requirements
  2. Assess privacy protections in destination country
  3. Implement enhanced contractual safeguards
  4. Consider additional technical protections (e.g., stronger de-identification)
  5. Establish ongoing monitoring and compliance procedures
