Appendix 1

About Synthetic Data

Comprehensive introduction to synthetic data concepts, generation methods, applications in health research, and foundational principles that underpin effective synthetic data governance frameworks.

Understanding synthetic data

Synthetic data is artificially generated information that mimics the statistical properties and patterns of real data without containing any actual personal information. In health research contexts, synthetic data enables analysis, testing, and development activities while significantly reducing privacy risks compared to using real patient data.

This appendix provides the foundational knowledge necessary to understand synthetic data applications, generation methods, and governance requirements that inform the SynD-DGF framework approach to responsible synthetic health data management.

1
What is synthetic data?

Synthetic data is computer-generated information designed to replicate the characteristics, patterns, and statistical properties of real datasets without containing any actual records from the original source. Unlike traditional de-identification approaches that modify real data, synthetic data generation creates entirely new records that preserve the utility of the original dataset while eliminating direct connections to real individuals.

Key characteristics of synthetic data:
  • Statistical fidelity: Preserves important statistical relationships and patterns from the source data
  • Privacy protection: Contains no actual personal information or real individual records
  • Functional utility: Supports intended analysis and research purposes
  • Controlled generation: Created through systematic processes with defined parameters
  • Scalable production: Can generate datasets of varying sizes and complexity

2
Synthetic data generation methods

Various approaches exist for generating synthetic data, each with different complexity, privacy guarantees, and utility preservation characteristics. The choice of generation method depends on the source data characteristics, intended use case, privacy requirements, and available technical resources.

Rule-based generation

Approach: Uses predefined rules and business logic to generate data based on known relationships and constraints.

Best for: Simple datasets with well-understood relationships and clear business rules.

Advantages: Transparent, interpretable, fast generation, deterministic output.

Limitations: Limited complexity, may not capture subtle patterns, requires domain expertise.

Statistical modelling

Approach: Fits statistical models to source data and generates new records by sampling from these models.

Best for: Datasets with clear statistical distributions and measurable relationships.

Advantages: Good statistical fidelity, interpretable models, formal privacy analysis possible.

Limitations: May struggle with complex relationships, assumes specific distributions.

Machine learning approaches

Approach: Trains machine learning models (neural networks, GANs, VAEs) to learn data patterns and generate new records.

Best for: Complex datasets with intricate patterns and high-dimensional data.

Advantages: Handles complex patterns, high fidelity for complex data, scalable.

Limitations: Black box nature, training complexity, potential overfitting risks.

Hybrid approaches

Approach: Combines multiple generation methods, often using statistical models for structure and ML for complex patterns.

Best for: Complex health datasets requiring both interpretability and pattern preservation.

Advantages: Balances interpretability and complexity, leverages strengths of multiple approaches.

Limitations: Implementation complexity, requires expertise in multiple methods.

Benefits and advantages

Synthetic data offers significant advantages over traditional approaches to health data sharing and analysis, particularly in addressing privacy concerns while maintaining research and analytical utility.

Privacy and security benefits:
  • No direct personal information: Eliminates individual-level privacy risks
  • Reduced re-identification potential: No direct mapping to real individuals
  • Controlled privacy guarantees: Formal privacy mechanisms can be incorporated
  • Simplified compliance: Easier regulatory navigation for many use cases
  • Reduced data breach impact: Lower consequences if synthetic data is compromised
Operational and technical benefits:
  • Scalable generation: Create datasets of any size as needed
  • Rapid availability: Generate data on demand without complex approval processes
  • Enhanced sharing: Easier collaboration across organisations and jurisdictions
  • Testing flexibility: Create edge cases and specific scenarios for testing
  • Reduced storage costs: Generate data when needed rather than storing large datasets
Research and development advantages:
  • Accelerated innovation: Faster development cycles without privacy barriers
  • Broader collaboration: Enable multi-party research without complex data sharing agreements
  • Proof-of-concept development: Test ideas and approaches before accessing real data
  • Training and education: Safe environment for learning and skill development
  • Algorithm development: Develop and test models without privacy constraints
5
Limitations and challenges

While synthetic data offers significant benefits, it also presents important limitations and challenges that must be carefully considered in any implementation. Understanding these constraints is essential for appropriate use and realistic expectations.

Quality and utility challenges
  1. Fidelity preservation: Maintaining statistical relationships while protecting privacy
  2. Rare event representation: Difficulty capturing uncommon but important patterns
  3. Temporal relationships: Preserving time-series and longitudinal patterns
  4. Complex correlations: Maintaining intricate multi-variable relationships
  5. Domain-specific patterns: Ensuring clinical plausibility and medical validity
Technical implementation challenges
  1. Model selection: Choosing appropriate generation methods for specific data types
  2. Parameter tuning: Balancing privacy protection with utility preservation
  3. Computational resources: Managing processing requirements for complex generation
  4. Quality assessment: Developing metrics to evaluate synthetic data adequacy
  5. Validation processes: Ensuring generated data meets intended use requirements
Governance and assurance challenges
  1. Privacy risk assessment: Quantifying and managing residual privacy risks
  2. Use case boundaries: Defining appropriate and inappropriate applications
  3. Quality standards: Establishing benchmarks for synthetic data quality
  4. Ongoing monitoring: Detecting and responding to quality degradation or misuse
  5. Stakeholder acceptance: Building confidence in synthetic data approaches
Key limitations to consider:
  • Utility trade-offs: Some utility loss is inevitable compared to real data
  • Generation complexity: High-quality synthetic data requires sophisticated methods
  • Domain expertise: Effective implementation requires deep understanding of data and domain
  • Validation burden: Extensive testing required to ensure fitness for purpose
  • Evolving field: Methods and best practices continue to develop rapidly

Synthetic data in the SynD-DGF framework context

The SynD-DGF framework recognises both the potential and the challenges of synthetic health data. The framework's five-step process addresses the key considerations identified in this appendix:

Framework alignment with synthetic data principles:
  • Step 1: Ensures appropriate use cases that leverage synthetic data benefits
  • Step 2: Addresses source data quality requirements for effective generation
  • Step 3: Provides guidance on generation method selection and implementation
  • Step 4: Systematically evaluates and manages privacy risks
  • Step 5: Establishes ongoing governance and monitoring processes
Framework responses to synthetic data challenges:
  • Quality assurance: Comprehensive assessment requirements and validation processes
  • Privacy protection: Multi-layered privacy risk assessment and mitigation
  • Governance integration: Clear roles, responsibilities, and oversight mechanisms
  • Use case boundaries: Explicit criteria for appropriate synthetic data applications
  • Continuous improvement: Feedback loops and learning from implementation experience

This foundational understanding of synthetic data concepts, methods, and considerations provides the basis for the detailed guidance and assessment processes outlined in the framework's subsequent steps and appendices.
