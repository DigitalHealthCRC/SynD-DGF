Impact assessment

This mandatory assessment evaluates the broader ethical, social, cultural, and sovereignty implications of your synthetic health data project to ensure responsible and appropriate use.

Comprehensive evaluation required

The accountable decision-maker must assess the synthetic data request in consideration of all the following aspects. This assessment applies to all synthetic data request types and must address potential impacts on individuals, communities, and stakeholders.

Assessment scope

This impact assessment covers:

  • Organisations requesting another organisation to generate synthetic data for specific projects
  • Organisations establishing synthetic datasets for multiple potential projects or purposes
  • Organisations or End Users requesting access to existing synthetic datasets for different purposes

Data ethics

Does the synthetic data use case raise concerns with regard to ethical considerations, in terms of the potential for harmful impacts on individuals, particular cohorts or sections of the community?

Data types that could raise ethical concerns include:

  • Health information (including physical and mental health)
  • Disability
  • Address or housing status
  • Alcohol and drug use
  • Sexuality
  • Data about children
  • Data about family violence / AVO's
  • Aboriginality
  • Ethnicity
  • Language background other than English
  • Religion
  • Trade union membership
  • Text-based fields / unstructured data
  • Staff information
  • Practice performance

Indigenous Data Sovereignty Requirements

Synthetic data use cases involving data about Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples are subject to additional considerations beyond the standard framework requirements. Indigenous Data Sovereignty principles must be respected and incorporated throughout the project lifecycle.

Indigenous Data Sovereignty principles

Indigenous Data Sovereignty is the right of Indigenous peoples to autonomously decide what, how and why Indigenous data and information are collected, accessed and used.

Ethical review requirements

For research about Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples, approval from a registered Aboriginal HREC is required.

Relevant Aboriginal HRECs include the Aboriginal Health & Medical Research Council (AH&MRC) or the Research Ethics Committee of the Australian Institute of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Studies (AIATSIS).
